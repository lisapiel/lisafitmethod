## Template A — The Frustration Pivot

**What it is:** Opens with someone doing everything "right" and getting nothing for it. Identifies the missing piece. Shows the contrast.

**Why it works:** Validates the audience's failed effort (it wasn't their fault — they had bad information). Creates enormous relief. Positions the insight as simple once you see it. This is the most-shared structure in women's fitness content because the audience has almost universally had this exact experience.

---

### The Formula

**Hook:**
> "I [did the right thing] for [specific time period]. I was [consistent/working hard/showing up]. [Disappointing result]. Then I discovered [specific insight]. Here's what actually changed."

**Body:**
- Beat 1 — The effort (show how hard they were working so the failure clearly isn't about laziness)
- Beat 2 — The result that didn't come (be specific and honest — vague disappointment doesn't land)
- Beat 3 — The turning point insight (name it precisely — "progressive overload," "movement quality," "structured programming" — not just "I found a better way")
- Beat 4 — The contrast (what changed when the insight was applied — keep this concrete and physical, not just emotional)
- Beat 5 — The implication for the audience (optional CTA or reflection question)

**CTA formula:**
> "If this sounds familiar, [specific action]."
> OR: "This is exactly what [program name] was built to teach."

---

### Real Example from Research

**Source:** GGS "My Switch to Strength" (girlsgonestrong.com)

Hook: Opens with packing four changes of workout clothes for a single Las Vegas day — a vivid sensory detail that communicates the extremity of the "before" without lecturing.

> "I was sick all of the friggin' time."
> "I was getting flabbier." (despite 5–6 hours daily of cardio)
> "There is nothing quite as glorious as beginner strength gains."

The turning point was a coach cutting ALL cardio and introducing resistance training. The contrast between "5–6 hours of cardio daily" and "75 minutes, 4 days/week strength training" with dramatically better results is the hook's payoff.

---

### Lisa's Adaptation

**Hook:**
> "For two years, I took every group fitness class I could find. I was consistent. I showed up. My body barely changed. Then I discovered what structure actually meant — and within months, everything was different."

**Body beats for Lisa:**
1. The effort: classes, cardio, group workouts — show up, work hard, be consistent
2. The result: "Years of effort without real progress. I wasn't lazy. I was just doing the wrong kind of work."
3. The turning point: "I found strength training. Real programming. Progressive overload. For the first time, I understood what I was actually doing and why."
4. The contrast: "My body started changing in a way it never had before. And I became obsessed with understanding why it was finally working."
5. The implication: "Most people who aren't getting results don't need to work harder. They need to work differently. That's what Training Foundations teaches."

---

### Best Platforms
1. TikTok / Instagram Reels (60–90 seconds, talking head or b-roll)
2. Instagram caption (single image)
3. Email subject line + body
4. YouTube video hook (first 90 seconds before diving deeper)

---

### Voice Guardrails

**USE:**
- "I was consistent. I was working hard." (validate the effort before naming the problem)
- "Structure," "programming," "progressive overload" (name the insight precisely)
- "I finally understood what I was actually doing and why" (the educational payoff)
- Specific timeframes and specific results — vague transformation claims undercut Lisa's credibility

**AVOID:**
- "I decided to get in shape" — sounds like the starting point was vanity
- "I wanted to tone up" — banned vocabulary
- "I transformed my body" — sounds like a before/after ad
- "I was frustrated" without specificity — every fitness brand uses this; Lisa's version needs concrete detail

**Engineering angle insert:** Beat 3 (the turning point). "My engineering background gave me a framework for understanding why this worked. Strength training isn't random — it's a system. Progressive overload is a principle with a mechanism behind it."

**Injury story insert:** Can replace Beat 1–2 OR be added as a Beat 2.5 ("And then my back gave out, which forced me to understand movement quality at a level I never would have otherwise"). Don't force it into every Frustration Pivot — save the full injury arc for Template D.

## Template B — The Education Unlock

**What it is:** Teaches one specific thing the audience doesn't know. Opens with a hook that signals insider information — something widely misunderstood or omitted from mainstream fitness advice. Delivers the insight, explains the mechanism, shows what correct looks like.

**Why it works:** People share educational content because sharing it makes THEM look smart. It's not about the creator — it's about the reader becoming more knowledgeable in front of their network. The more counterintuitive the insight, the more shareable it is.

---

### The Formula

**Hook options:**
> "Nobody tells beginners [counterintuitive truth about training]."
> "Things I wish I knew when I started lifting."
> "The reason you're not making progress in the gym isn't [what everyone says]. It's [actual reason]."
> "[Number] things your fitness app isn't teaching you."

**Body:**
- Beat 1 — Name the mistake or gap (specific, not vague: not "people skip the basics" but "people add weight before they can hinge correctly")
- Beat 2 — Explain why it's common (not judgment — systemic: "fitness apps give you workouts, not education")
- Beat 3 — The correct principle (name it, define it simply, give the mechanism)
- Beat 4 — What it looks like in practice (concrete example from a movement, a workout, a progression)
- Beat 5 — What changes when you get this right (result/outcome)

**CTA formula:**
> "This is one of [X] foundations I teach in [program name]."
> OR: "If you want the full breakdown, [link/resource]."

---

### Real Example from Research

**Source:** TikTok pattern confirmed by Viryze research as top-performing format

The "Things I wish I knew when I started" hook is one of the highest-performing formats in fitness TikTok because it activates two emotions simultaneously: (1) aspiration (I want to know this too) and (2) nostalgia/reflection (I can share this to help others).

**Source:** Caroline Girvan reviews consistently cite movement education as a gap

> "[EPIC] provides no coaching cues, no form corrections, no guidance on 'how heavy should I go?'"

The audience watching Girvan WANTS this education. It's a market signal that Lisa directly fills.

---

### Lisa's Adaptation

**Hook:**
> "Nobody tells beginner women this about strength training: more workouts isn't the answer. Structure is. Here's what the difference actually looks like."

OR for a specific movement version:
> "3 things your body is doing wrong in a squat — and why fixing them changes everything else you do in the gym."

**Body beats for Lisa:**
1. The mistake: "Most beginners choose programs by how hard the workouts look. That's like choosing a building by its facade. The foundation determines everything."
2. Why it's common: "Fitness apps give you workouts. They don't give you a system. You get told WHAT to do. Nobody teaches you WHY — so when it stops working, you have no idea what to change."
3. The principle: "Progressive overload: your body adapts to a stimulus. Add a little more stress (weight, reps, sets) and it adapts again. Without this, you work hard and go nowhere."
4. In practice: "A structured program tracks your numbers week over week. Week 1 you squat 45 lbs for 3×8. Week 3 you squat 55 lbs for 3×10. That's not just harder — that's specific. That specificity is what drives change."
5. The outcome: "When you understand this, every workout has a purpose. You stop guessing. You start building."

**CTA:**
> "This is Module 1 of Training Foundations. If you want all 5 foundation principles explained like this, that's what the program is."

---

### Best Platforms
1. Instagram carousel (6–10 slides — each slide = one beat)
2. YouTube video (3–8 minutes of teaching)
3. Blog post (long-form, SEO-ranked)
4. Email newsletter (education + subtle CTA)
5. TikTok (compressed, 60–90 second version)

---

### Voice Guardrails

**USE:**
- "Here's the mechanism behind why this works" (Lisa's engineering lens)
- Specific exercise names and numbers (45 lbs, 3×8, 3×10)
- "Most programs give you workouts. This gives you a system."
- "The reason" rather than "the secret" — Lisa doesn't do secrets; she does explanations

**AVOID:**
- "Secret hack" / "nobody wants you to know" — conspiracy framing conflicts with Lisa's education-first credibility
- "Easy way to" — Lisa's value is understanding, not shortcuts
- Numbered "tips" lists without mechanisms — tips are forgettable; mechanisms are teachable

**Engineering angle insert:** Beat 2–3 always. "My engineering background taught me to ask: what's the mechanism? Why does this produce results? Random workouts fail the mechanism test — there's no underlying principle driving adaptation."

**Injury story insert:** Optional in Beat 4. "After my back injury, I understood movement quality at a different level. A hip hinge done wrong isn't just inefficient — it's how you hurt yourself. Here's what correct looks like."

## Template C — The Credibility Contrast

**What it is:** Uses an unusual background, credential, or lens to reframe a fitness topic in a way that challenges conventional wisdom. Positions Lisa as a different KIND of thinker, not just a different trainer.

**Why it works:** In a market saturated with CPTs, the engineering background is genuinely differentiating. People share this content because it makes them feel like they found a smarter perspective in a noisy space. The "contrast" structure (here's what [group] does vs. here's what [different lens] reveals) creates intellectual tension that drives engagement.

---

### The Formula

**Hook:**
> "Here's what [unusual background] taught me about [fitness topic] that most trainers miss."
> "I built my training program like an engineer, not an influencer. Here's the difference."
> "Two engineering master's degrees taught me one thing about fitness: [counterintuitive insight]."

**Body:**
- Beat 1 — Establish the lens (engineering/systems thinking) in one or two sentences — don't over-explain the background
- Beat 2 — Apply the lens to a specific training concept (not training in general — one specific thing)
- Beat 3 — Show what conventional wisdom gets wrong about that concept
- Beat 4 — Show what the engineering/systems view reveals instead
- Beat 5 — The outcome of applying this view (personal or general)

**CTA formula:**
> "This is the framework behind Training Foundations — a program built the way an engineer would build it, not the way an influencer would sell it."

---

### Real Example from Research

**Source:** Casey Johnston "She's A Beast" — contrast framing

Casey Johnston's newsletter name itself is a contrast ("Beast" = reclaimed from pejorative). Her entire brand is built on applying a different lens (anti-diet, strength-focused) to a topic everyone was framing wrong (women + fitness = shrink yourself).

The contrast structure: "Here's what the fitness industry tells women to do. Here's what actually makes sense." This contrast creates a reader who feels like they've found the truth after years of being misled.

---

### Lisa's Adaptation

**Short-form version (TikTok/Reel):**

> "I built my training program like an engineer, not an influencer. An influencer gives you workouts. An engineer gives you a system. The difference: workouts tell you what to do. A system tells you why — and what to do when it stops working. Training Foundations is the system."

**Long-form carousel version (Instagram, 6 slides):**

Slide 1 (Hook): "I have two master's degrees in engineering. This is what systems thinking revealed about why most women never make progress in the gym."
Slide 2: "Engineers don't build structures without foundations. Fitness programs do this constantly — jumping straight to intensity before establishing the base."
Slide 3: "Engineers think in inputs and outputs. Stimulus → adaptation → result. Random workouts break this chain. You can't predict an output without controlling the input."
Slide 4: "Engineers design for failure modes. What happens when you miss a workout? When you plateau? The program needs an answer. Most programs don't have one."
Slide 5: "Engineers version their work. Week 1 is different from Week 4 on purpose. Progressive overload isn't motivation — it's engineering."
Slide 6 (CTA): "Training Foundations was built with this framework. Not a collection of workouts. A system designed to produce a specific output: a body that understands itself."

---

### Best Platforms
1. Instagram carousel (highest engagement-to-effort ratio)
2. YouTube Short (30 seconds — compressed version)
3. TikTok (talking head, 45–75 seconds)
4. Twitter/X thread

---

### Voice Guardrails

**USE:**
- Engineering vocabulary applied to fitness: "system," "inputs and outputs," "stimulus → adaptation," "progressive overload as a principle"
- "I built this" — ownership language; Lisa didn't discover this from a guru; she built it
- Contrast as structure: "Influencer gives you X. Engineer gives you Y." (parallel construction)

**AVOID:**
- Over-explaining the engineering background (one or two sentences MAX — the audience cares about the insight, not the résumé)
- Making this feel like bragging about credentials — the engineering background is a LENS, not a flex
- Abstract systems talk without a concrete fitness example — always land on something tangible

**Injury story insert:** NOT in this template unless it reinforces the systems point. "After my back injury, I rebuilt everything from first principles — movement mechanics, mobility, load progression. That's what an engineer does when a structure fails."

## Template D — The Comeback Arc

**What it is:** The full narrative arc: opens at the moment of failure or collapse (not the happy ending), builds through the discovery and rebuild, closes with the outcome — and makes the implication clear for the audience.

**Why it works:** This is the most emotionally resonant structure in all of content. It works because it follows the same arc as every story humans have ever loved: tension → discovery → resolution. It creates empathy (the audience sees themselves in the struggle), hope (the resolution is real and earned), and trust (the creator isn't selling from strength — they're sharing from experience).

This is Lisa's most powerful template because her story is genuinely extraordinary — injury that lasted a year, complete rebuild from zero, came back stronger and more knowledgeable than before. She should not underuse this.

---

### The Formula

**Critical rule:** Start at the collapse, not the journey. Do NOT open with "I want to tell you about a time I got hurt." Open IN the moment of pain.

**Hook (open at collapse):**
> "About four years ago, I couldn't get out of bed without pain. My back had given out. I'd been training for three years and thought I knew what I was doing."

**Body:**
- Beat 1 — The collapse in full (the pain, the loss, what it cost)
- Beat 2 — The forced stop and the question it created ("I had to ask: what did I actually know? And the honest answer was: less than I thought.")
- Beat 3 — The rebuild (what was discovered, what was learned — be specific: movement mechanics, warm-up protocols, mobility, real programming logic)
- Beat 4 — The return (what came back differently — not just pain-free, but more capable, more knowledgeable, more structurally sound)
- Beat 5 — The extraction (what this means for the audience who isn't injured but might be on the same path)
- Beat 6 — The offer/CTA (optional but natural: "Training Foundations is what I wish I had before any of this happened")

**CTA formula:**
> "I built Training Foundations so you don't have to learn this the way I did."
> "This is what I teach in the program — not because I studied it, but because I had to rebuild myself with it."

---

### Real Examples from Research

**Source 1:** Casey Johnston NPR story
> "After years spent running and dieting, Johnston stumbled on a Reddit post about weightlifting that transformed her relationship with exercise."
The word "stumbled" is important — it suggests the discovery was accidental, which makes it feel more authentic than "I researched and found."

**Source 2:** Nellie Barnett story (Women's Health)
> "I just remember locking in with each deadlift…when I did, it was a 'wow' moment of, 'Oh, I really did that — 285 pounds!'"
The comeback arc climax is a single specific moment — not a montage of progress, but one moment of realization. Find Lisa's equivalent specific moment.

**Source 3:** GGS Jen cardio story
> "There is nothing quite as glorious as beginner strength gains."
The payoff line should be unexpected — not "I finally got results" but a more specific, honest reflection on what it actually felt like.

### Lisa's Adaptation (Full Version — YouTube / Email)

**[OPEN — IN THE PAIN]**

About four years ago, my back gave out.

Not a minor tweak. The kind of pain where getting out of bed in the morning required a plan. Where sitting at a desk was something I had to manage. Where training — something I'd been doing seriously for three years — came to a complete stop.

**[THE QUESTION]**

I had time to think during that year. And what I kept coming back to was: I thought I knew what I was doing. I was training consistently. I was adding weight. I was getting stronger. But I'd skipped the entire foundation.

Warm-ups felt like wasted time. Mobility work wasn't on my radar. I hadn't learned movement mechanics — I'd just learned to lift heavier.

When the structure has no foundation, eventually something gives.

**[THE REBUILD]**

I rebuilt everything from the ground up. Not just the exercises — the whole framework. Movement mechanics first. Warm-up protocols. Mobility. Real programming logic. Why progressive overload works. What the hip hinge actually is. How the core stabilizes, not just contracts.

I became a certified personal trainer — not because I wanted a credential, but because I needed to understand what I had actually been missing.

**[THE RETURN]**

I came back pain-free. And stronger than I had ever been. Not because I trained harder — because I trained on a foundation that could hold weight.

**[THE EXTRACTION]**

You don't have to go through what I went through to get what I learned.

Most people are training on an incomplete foundation right now. They're consistent. They're working hard. They might even be seeing results. But at some point — too much weight too soon, a movement done wrong for too long — something gives.

Training Foundations is what I wish I'd had at the start. The five movement patterns every strong body is built on. The coaching cues I had to learn the hard way. A four-week structured program designed to build the base first, so that everything you add after actually sticks.

**[CTA]**
You can learn this the way I did — through injury and a year of rebuilding. Or you can start here.

## Template E — The Specific Claim

**What it is:** The most compressed template. Numbers, specifics, no filler. Every word earns its place. Designed for ads, email subject lines, short-form video captions, and anywhere you have three seconds to make an impression.

**Why it works:** In a space full of vague transformation promises, specificity cuts through. "Get results" is noise. "4 weeks. 3 days. 50+ videos. $47. One time." is signal. The audience can evaluate a specific claim. They can't evaluate a vague promise.

---

### The Formula

**Core structure:**
> "[Number]. [Timeframe]. [Specific feature or action]. [Specific result or differentiator]."

Repeat the pattern 3–4 times. No transitions. No filler. Each line is a complete claim.

**Closing line options:**
> "No subscription. No guesswork. One foundation."
> "This is Training Foundations."
> "Learn the foundations once. Use them forever."

---

**Natacha Océane:** "260+ videos demonstrating correct form." — The specificity of "260+" is more credible than "hundreds of."

**Lisa's own copy (already on site):** "Four weeks. Three days a week. Built-in warm-ups and cool-downs. Progressive overload system included." — This is already Template E. It just needs to be deployed more aggressively in ads and short-form.

### Voice Guardrails

**USE:**
- Real numbers ($47, not "affordable"; 50+, not "many"; 4 weeks, not "a few weeks")
- "One time" over "lifetime access" — "one time" is the value claim; "lifetime access" is the feature
- Comparison framing without naming competitors directly ("other apps charge $20/month" is enough)

**AVOID:**
- Explaining the numbers — Template E is the claim; explanations belong in other templates
- Adding emotional language — "Transform your body in just 4 weeks!" breaks the template by adding hype
- Hedging: "up to 50 videos" or "approximately 4 weeks" — specific claims require specific numbers

**Engineering angle insert:** NOT in Template E. The engineering angle requires context. Template E is pure signal.

**Injury story insert:** NOT in Template E. The injury story requires emotion and space. Template E is no space.

## Voice Guardrails: Master List

Apply these to every piece of content before publishing.

### The 3-Question Voice Check
1. **Would an influencer say this?** If yes, revise. Lisa is an educator, not a hype machine.
2. **Does it explain WHY, not just WHAT?** Every claim needs a mechanism. "Progressive overload works" is a claim. "Progressive overload works because your body adapts to stimulus — more stimulus = more adaptation" is education.
3. **Does it respect the audience's intelligence?** No dumbing down. Lisa's audience chose her because she doesn't talk to them like they need to be motivated; they need to be informed.

### Banned Vocabulary
| Banned word/phrase | Why | Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| tone / toned / toning | Implies aesthetic goal as primary; pseudo-scientific | "build strength," "build muscle" |
| shred / shredded | Bro-fitness vocabulary | "lean out," "change body composition" |
| quick fix / in just X days | Hype and false promise | "4 weeks" with no qualifier on "quick" |
| transformation | Overused sales word; implies before = bad | "progress," "change," "building a foundation" |
| girl boss / boss babe | Cringe; also trivializes | Never |
| effortless / easy | Lies to the customer | "manageable," "three days a week" |
| secret / hack | Conspiracy framing | "principle," "mechanism," "what actually works" |

### Required Vocabulary / Framings
| Required framing | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| "structure," "system," "foundation" | Lisa's core positioning words — repeat them |
| "progressive overload" | The key principle Lisa teaches — use it by name |
| "movement quality" | Differentiates from volume-only programs |
| "built to last" | The brand's core promise — use it |
| "smart training" | Lisa's differentiator vs. "effective" (too generic) |
| $47 framed as a choice, not a discount | Price is a structural decision that signals respect |

### The Price Rule
**$47 is never an apology.** Never say "for only $47" or "just $47." Say:
> "One-time $47. No subscription."
> "$47. One time. No ongoing cost."
> "Less than three months of any subscription app. Once."

The contrast to subscription models ($144/year for Meg Squats, $240/year for Kayla) is Lisa's competitive pricing advantage AND a values statement about not making customers dependent on ongoing payment.
